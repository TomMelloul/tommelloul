ANIMISM

The Living City

Nitsan Bartov, Tom Melloul
BisTAU Studio led by Arch. Moti Bodek, 2019
As we enter the 21st century, humanity’s striving for stability is being replaced by a constant desire for change and stimulation.

The built environment ironically reflects the gap between these desires, and is increasingly becoming more rigid and lifeless; a salient example of which is Netanya’s Ir Yamim district.

This project presents a new way of reading space through people; The city lives, adapts, and reacts to an ever-changing reality. It is dynamic and relevant, encouraging a sense of empathy between the city and its people.

ANIMISM is the birth of the living city.

    • EVO

    Living Evolution

    Eka Janashia, Tom Melloul
    Residential Studio led by Arch. Leonardo Kelijman & Arch. Dafna Matok, 2017
    Urban environments have seen immense growth in population density in recent decades, leading to an extensive Vertical Expansion of the urban residential building

    Following major cities around the world, Tel-Aviv, Israel's financial and cultural capital, has increasingly adopted verticality in the new millennium. While some areas have adapted well to linear extrusion, others became secluded and have essentially detached themselves from Tel-Aviv’s urban DNA, while depriving their inhabitants of recreational communal zones. In light of this phenomenon, we set out to examine an alternative urban expansion method which will allow for an efficient yet communal living - a new Spatially Expandable urban model.

    To lay the foundations for a case study, we first attempted to determine the city's core DNA by examining Geddes’ original urban plan for Tel-Aviv. The plan's most notable urban factor and the recurrent theme was its array of Green Spaces - a quintessential Tel-Avivian urban generator. Recognizing their importance, we have pinpointed and interconnected each green space to create a new way to read the city - an alternative Voronoi map.

    After performing an urban study via the new map, we then zoomed in to a typical Geddes lot and its surrounding buildings, which acted as a case study for our analysis. The analysis led us to extract 5 key Gedessian core parameters which formed the basis for a new spatially expandable model.

    We then converted the 2D subdivision logic into spatial tesselation, which resulted in a double-layered cellular mesh based on a 3D point cloud. The mesh cells, however, were inhabitable mainly due to their sloped floors. Therefore, we further manipulated the point cloud and created a horizontal coplanar tesselation based system.

    Ultimately, the final spatial model produced a porous cellular network that could expand and contract accordingly. Each geometrically unique cell contained a horizontal floor and followed core "Gedessian" values. The cells could interchangeably act as dwelling units, open spaces, or alternatively merge with neighboring cells to accommodate for larger-scale recreational and commercial activities - giving birth to EVO - an adaptive living system that stems from Tel-Aviv's unique urban DNA.

    • MIST

    Talpiot Bath House

    Noa Eisenberg, Tom Melloul
    Interior Design Studio led by Arch. Guy Re Moor, 2018
    Talpiot market (Arch. Moshe Gerstel, 1940) has long served as one of Haifa’s most influential cultural landmarks. Alas, this fine specimen of the International Style that once housed a bustling market has deteriorated since the 80's to near-abandonment in recent years.

    Despite its current disfunction and physical state, the structure’s worn-out components tell a mystical story of constant transformation, and its symmetric layout beares a striking resemblance to ancient classical bathhouse floorplans. Indeed, bathhouses, alongside their obvious functional elements, have always seemed to possess mysterious qualities that greatly contributed to their very essence of being.

    Stagnant water, crumbling walls, damp concrete, and broken glass - these, and many more, contribute to a Talpiot's aura of patina that encapsulates its concrete shell. The building's aesthetics of ruin and decay captivates one’s imagination, as it becomes one with nature’s elements. Talpiot's materials bind with water, light, and vegetation to ultimately transcend into metamaterials - laying the foundations for MIST - Talpiot’s Bathhouse.

    • UMAGUTI

    "Where There's Life"

    Nitsan Bartov, Yagev Langer, Tom Melloul
    Azrieli Global Studio led by Arch. Tamir Lavie, 2016
    The Azrieli Global Studio Program is a collaboration between McGill University, The Technion, and Tel Aviv University.

    The 6-week course was an immersive design studio which included an excursion to the Canadian Subarctic. The students researched ideas of limits and extremities in the global natural terrain, focusing on the freezing and remote landscape of Schefferville, Quebec - the field trip's destination.

    Northern Subarctic Canada is plentiful with minerals, most notably Iron Ore. Over the past decades, the resource-abundant area has seen the mining industry cement its role as a relatively exclusive economic driving force. However, lax government regulations have allowed for unattended and overly intense mining, leaving behind barren and wounded soil, and a struggling, neglected, local community.

    Alongside rising financial and social issues, recent researches have also shown a decline in the local population's physical and mental health, due to insufficient intake of fruits and vegetables. These were reaffirmed upon interviewing local teenagers, during which they repeatedly mentioned fresh fruits and vegetables when asked what life in this area lacks the most.

    UMAGUTI (Innu for "Where There's Life"), sets out to put abandoned mines to use and attempt to heal the soil's open wounds by inhabiting them. Through parametric design, solar form-finding, and advanced building technologies, UMAGUTI utilizes the mine's unique footprint. The terrace-like topography, combined with a robust lightweight ETFE Membrane, is used to accommodate for durable housing and sustainable sub-arctic farming that enables the cultivation of fresh local produce.

    The farm layout also includes a research facility and a visitor's center, aimed to provide the community with a set of robust tools that will help them grow, develop, and prosper by re-using discarded land.

    • CLEAR STUDIO

    A pop-up urban music studio

    Tom Melloul
    Technology Studio B led by Simone Friedman, 2016
    The Technology Studio focuses on the integrated design of a small structure with an emphasis on building technologies, materials, and design details. During the first part of the semester, the students designed a simple public toilets structure in large groups. During the 2nd part of the semester, each student was assigned to define and design a detachable pop-up function that will attach to the toilets anchor structure.

    The CLEAR STUDIO was designed as a traveling urban pop-up studio that can attach to each of the city's anchor public toilets structures. The studio's transparency allows for the participation of residents and passers-by alike and can enable the exposure of up and coming musicians. The constant change of the studio's location could contribute to a variety of urban activities (such as Tel Aviv's yearly "Book Week", Marathons, Rallies, etc.) and can also act as a generator for cultural activity in impoverished areas of the city.

    • Health Sciences Building

    Urban layout and planning for multiple Educational Buildings

    Client: Yavne Municipality, Israel.
    Project led by Arch. Alessya Tonetti, Tom Melloul, Bea Architects & Town Planners, 2017
    In recent years, The Israeli health field suffered an increasing shortage of academically trained personnel in hospitals and community clinics. As a prominent leader in higher education, The Academic College of Tel Aviv-Yaffo sought to deal with this issue by establishing a new building for Health Sciences.

    Our studio was commissioned to design an innovative, technologically advanced new building in a challenging footprint, alongside producing an extensive brochure for potential donors.

    The Health Sciences Building's design aims to fundamentally connect and respond to its environment to promote efficiency and health. Rather than approaching the building as a “sealed box,” the Building’s envelope and materials were designed to accommodate Jaffa’s local weather and the site’s natural proximity to the seaside.

    During the design process, several of the core decisions focused on our ability to harness the building's natural ventilation potential, given its potential ability to substantially reduce energy use and improve thermal comfort. The design itself utilized innovative energy modeling to allow for a more accurate prediction of the natural ventilation potential of the local environment. By utilizing a parametric design process and advanced simulations we were able to merge an ecological approach with the same level of accuracy as a mechanically driven structure.
